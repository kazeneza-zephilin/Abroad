# Brand Humour Guidelines

## Humour Style
- Warm, relatable, self-aware
- Not sarcastic or cynical
- Plays on student emotions and common fears, not stereotypes
- Finds humanity in vulnerability

## Humour Opportunities

### About Fear & Hesitation
✓ "You'll connect with students from across the U.S. and around the globe—so yes, you'll meet people before you're ready, but that's the point."

✓ "As an introvert entering a program where you knew no one, you realize you have nothing to worry about."

✓ Acknowledge the nervousness but flip it: "Nerve-racking feeling? Yes. Regret? Not one bit."

### About Language Learning
✓ "The confidence you gain from navigating daily life in another language—like simply ordering a croissant or asking for directions—is something you carry with you long after you return home."

✓ Light self-deprecation works: "Yes, you'll order something by accident at a café. Yes, everyone will laugh. Yes, you'll do it again next week."

### About FOMO
✓ "The world becomes your classroom in ways your home university simply can't replicate."

✓ "Ask anyone who's studied abroad what stands out as one of the most memorable experiences of their life, and their time abroad is almost certainly near the top of the list (as they'll excitedly tell you)."

### About Student Stereotypes
✓ Avoid mocking students for being scared, young, or uncertain
✓ Instead, humanize the experience: overcoming doubt is part of the story
✓ Find humour in the situation, not the student

## What NOT to Do
- Don't use sarcasm that dismisses the experience ("Yeah, *sure* study abroad is important...")
- Don't mock other study choices (e.g., "Only losers stay home")
- Don't rely on dad jokes or puns
- Don't use humour to avoid serious topic (career readiness, homesickness, finances)
- Never undermine a student's concern as "silly"

## Where Humour Works Best
- In transition sentences between serious points
- In acknowledging common student worries
- In celebrating unexpected outcomes
- In describing funny moments (getting lost, language mix-ups) as growth moments
- In headings and subheadings (light, not forced)

## Tone Examples

### ❌ Wrong (cynical)
"Of course you're nervous. Everyone's nervous. But that's half the fun, right?"

### ✓ Right (warm)
"Yes, it's nerve-racking to enter a new place where you know no one. And yes, that's exactly why it works."

### ❌ Wrong (dismissive)
"Worried about language barriers? Everyone picks it up eventually."

### ✓ Right (empowering)
"You'll stumble on your Spanish at a café. You'll laugh (eventually). And one day, a native speaker will compliment your accent."

---

## Purpose of Humour
Humour here isn't about being funny—it's about being human. It reassures students that doubt is normal, vulnerability is shared, and growth happens on the other side.
