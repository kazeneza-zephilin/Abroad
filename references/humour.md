# Brand Humor Guidelines

## Humor Style
- **Self-deprecating**: About yourself, not the country or its people
- **Observational**: Noticing the funny parts of travel and communication
- **Subtle**: Make people smile, not laugh out loud
- **Never mocking**: Never at the expense of local people or culture
- **Honest**: Acknowledge the absurd without dismissing it

## Examples from Our Brand

### Self-Deprecating Humor
"I recognize him by his half right ear. When said like that I am sure I sound ghoulish, but you'll understand that I am still a journalist with a law degree."

**Why it works:** The speaker is aware of how the observation sounds, and gently pokes fun at their own situation. No one is actually being mocked.

---

### Language-Barrier Humor
"My basic French comes in handy. I take off my helmet and scream at the helmet in front of me. 'Je veux du café.'"

**Why it works:** The image of screaming basic French while on a moto is inherently funny. It's relatable. No one is mocked—we all do this when traveling.

---

### Observational Absurdity
The waitress asks multiple times: "Sure, you are not a student?" after the traveler has explained they're a writer.

"I tell her about my Congo visit and she is surprised if not mildly shocked. 'You went to Congo? It is not very safe in Congo... Are you a student?'"

**Why it works:** The repetition is funny without being mean. The waitress is just genuinely curious. We recognize the pattern.

---

### Overthinking Humor
"Learning French on the go has become an absolute necessity, a task, I hope can be attended to through google translate for Kinyarwanda, the national language of Rwanda is beyond me. I am nervous, given what I know about this country and I want to bust that myth from my mind. Will they laugh at my botched attempt at speaking French?"

**Why it works:** The internal spiral of anxiety is relatable. Anyone who's traveled has felt this. It's funny because it's honest.

---

### The Moto Meter Moment
"My moto-meter stands at 8,000Rwf (about $8). I add two dollars to the total."

**Why it works:** The specific detail and casual transaction is funny. It's a small, honest moment.

---

## What NOT to do
- Don't mock Rwandan English or French
- Don't make fun of traditions or customs
- Don't use tribal references or comparisons
- Don't make disability jokes (like the half-ear reference without context)
- Don't punch down at service workers
- Don't use shock humor or sex jokes
- Don't do self-deprecation about being a foreigner unwelcome

## What TO do
- Notice the funny parts of cultural differences
- Be honest about your own confusion or mistakes
- Acknowledge the absurdity of travel situations
- Use humor to build connection, not create distance
- Make the reader feel like an insider, not an outsider
- Use specific details to make situations relatable
- Self-aware humor about your assumptions
